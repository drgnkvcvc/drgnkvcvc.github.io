Scrum and fixed date contracts? Not a good idea.

Tweet
I have witnessed quite a few talks whether Scrum can or should be used for projects with fixed date contracts. In real-world software project contract, two things are always strictly specified – scope and date. However, quality is usually left out.
There are two main reasons for leaving out quality. First, it is really hard to specify it. Can it be measured by the number of bugs, or by the severity of the bug? Unless the bug is blocking the usage of the software, bug severity is something that is prone to discussion, therefore, hard to strictly specify in the contract.
This is the reason why projects with fixed date often tend to fulfill the scope by deadline, but quality is the first to suffer. Those who argue that you can use Scrum to deliver projects by the fixed date suggest negotiating the scope instead of quality with your client.
What if the whole scope is really crucial for business? We can cite the famous Pareto principle, but truthfully that principle can be applied to a project that has a vast amount of features. What if you need to deliver a small invoicing system within two months? In real life, invoicing features don’t really function without vendors, and vice-versa. On the other hand, if you have a vast amount of features, rock-stat negotiators and understanding clients, you might consider negotiating the scope cut. Hovewer, that trick doesn't usually work twice.
You can benefit tremendously by providing an estimate that is real, instead of an estimate that your clients want to hear.
One of the great benefits of Scrum is improving the quality and getting feedback as soon as possible. Deliver an MVP to your client as early and as often as you can. You should do so not just to ensure that you are building in the right direction, but also to build up trust. “I don’t care to see the first build of my product”, said nobody ever.
In case of fixed date contracts, try using some techniques to break down the scope better, plan more precisely, and get to know your teams velocity better. Do not be afraid to voice out concerns early, and place buffers just-in-case. You can benefit tremendously by providing an estimate that is real, instead of an estimate that your clients want to hear.
